# Project Vision

## Fantasy promise

The player should feel that Gensokyo is not a theme park of famous characters. It is a place with routines, pride, grudges, obligations, food, weather, rumor, and private habits. Romance emerges because the player becomes part of those routines.

The strongest romantic reward is not a confession. It is a tiny change in a character's ordinary behavior:

- Reimu leaves a cushion beside her without commenting on it.
- Marisa stops pretending an experiment was “nothing” and asks for help.
- Sakuya allows one unfinished task to remain unfinished.
- Youmu trusts another person to watch the garden.
- Aya lowers the camera.
- Kaguya chooses a finite evening instead of an endless diversion.

## Creative pillars

### 1. Monochrome is a mechanic, not a filter
The 1-bit world communicates focus, memory, truth, and omission. Dithering represents uncertainty. Clean white space represents emotional honesty. Dense black areas represent rumor, fear, or unspoken motive.

### 2. Every location changes the rules
The player should identify a place from its mechanics before reading its name. The shrine is about balance and chores; the mansion is about timing and etiquette; the mountain is about wind, hierarchy, and information; Eientei is about loops and unreliable perception; Hakugyokurou is about rhythm, appetite, and things that do not quite pass on.

### 3. Character truth outranks the joke
A fan meme may start a scene, but it cannot be the whole character. The ending beat must reconnect to a canon-compatible motive, responsibility, fear, or relationship.

### 4. Romance is reciprocal participation
The protagonist is not a collector of trophies. Progress requires learning how each character gives and receives trust. The ensemble route exists only when the player repeatedly respects boundaries, repairs conflict, and refuses to rank people for spectacle.

### 5. Short, replayable incidents
Most spot events should last 6–18 minutes. A player can finish one meaningful event in a short session, while route chains create a longer emotional arc.

### 6. Mechanical comedy
The joke should often happen through play:
- a donation box minigame becomes a bullet-hell accounting audit;
- a formal tea service becomes a frame-perfect time-control puzzle;
- a ghost banquet becomes an inventory survival game;
- an interview becomes a photo-graze duel.

## Audience

- Touhou fans who enjoy character-driven doujin works
- players who like compact narrative adventures
- retro pixel-art players
- players intimidated by full-scale bullet hell but interested in its language
- bilingual fans who want Japanese and English presentation

## Tone

Warm, dry, mischievous, occasionally melancholy. The game may tease characters, but it must not humiliate them for existing. The player can be the butt of jokes without becoming a blank punching bag.

## Non-goals

- reproducing the visual density or difficulty of an official Touhou shooter
- simulating every canonical ability at universe-breaking scale
- replacing official works as a lore encyclopedia
- using explicit sexual content
- presenting common fanon as fact
- shipping with unlicensed official music or artwork
- building seventy full romance routes for v1.0

## Vertical-slice success test

A new player who knows nothing about Touhou should understand:
- who Reimu and Marisa are in this story;
- why the shrine feels different from the forest;
- why grazing is satisfying;
- why a small domestic gesture can be a relationship reward.

A longtime fan should recognize:
- canon-compatible speech and priorities;
- an affectionate but restrained use of fandom jokes;
- mechanics derived from character identity rather than generic skins.
