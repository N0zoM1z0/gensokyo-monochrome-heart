# Narrative Bible

## Logline

Months after a dreamlike separation from Gensokyo, an ordinary Outside World human follows an old Hakurei charm through a broken boundary. They return to find Gensokyo rendered in black and white—not because color has vanished, but because shared memories are being flattened into rumors. To prevent the world from becoming a perfect but lifeless archive, the protagonist must revisit places, help people perform their everyday lives, and decide which memories deserve to remain private.

## Central incident

**The Monochrome Memory Incident**

A hidden mechanism is converting lived memories into stable, searchable “records.” The process is attractive:
- no one forgets;
- rumors become consistent;
- painful ambiguity disappears;
- history becomes easy to govern.

But the cost is severe:
- private gestures become public symbols;
- contradictory memories are erased;
- relationships become fixed roles;
- places lose the small irregularities that make them alive.

The true antagonist is not a single villain. It is the desire—shared by sages, historians, reporters, lonely immortals, and even the protagonist—to make fragile happiness permanent.

## Thematic questions

- Is a memory still intimate when everyone can inspect it?
- Can an ordinary human matter to beings who will outlive them?
- Is permanence the same as care?
- What is the ethical difference between recording a person and understanding them?
- Can an ensemble relationship remain honest without turning people into options?
- What does it mean to return to a place that may have been a dream?

## Protagonist

The player names the protagonist and selects pronouns. They are an adult from the Outside World.

They begin with:
- no combat superiority;
- one faded Hakurei charm;
- a small handmade doll;
- an impossible cherry petal;
- practical domestic skills;
- incomplete memories of an earlier Gensokyo year.

Their arc is from **being grateful merely to return** to **accepting responsibility for how their presence changes the community**.

## Narrative tone

The story alternates:
- dry domestic comedy;
- social misunderstanding;
- ritualized combat;
- quiet conversations at thresholds: verandas, gates, kitchens, bridges, corridors, shorelines.

No scene should remain at maximum intensity for long. A large set piece earns a low-volume afterbeat.

## Truth structure

The story never forces a single answer to “Was the Outside World arc real?”

Three statements can all remain true:
1. The protagonist remembers it.
2. physical keepsakes exist.
3. boundaries and dreams do not obey ordinary proof.

The finale resolves emotional responsibility, not metaphysics.

## Antagonistic pressures

### Archive pressure
Akyuu and Kosuzu recognize the value and danger of total records.

### Newspaper pressure
Aya and Hatate compete over what the public deserves to know.

### Boundary pressure
Yukari wants Gensokyo protected but is tempted by a memory system that could stabilize it.

### Eternality pressure
Kaguya and Eirin understand the appeal of preserving moments indefinitely.

### Order pressure
Eiki, Kanako, Remilia, and Miko each have reasons to prefer legible systems.

### Player pressure
The player may unconsciously want every route, every answer, and every private scene permanently available.

## Emotional rule

The final solution is not to destroy memory. It is to restore **consent, context, and forgetting**:
- some memories become public chronicles;
- some remain personal;
- some are allowed to fade;
- some can be revisited only with the people involved.

## Ending families

### Friendship endings
Deep companionship without romance.

### Route endings
A promise shaped by the character's relationship to time, duty, and privacy.

### Ensemble Accord
Multiple relationships continue through explicit negotiation and shared responsibilities.

### Chronicle ending
The player prioritizes public truth and becomes a keeper of records.

### Dream ending
The player returns Outside, retaining only a few private objects.

### Monochrome ending
A cautionary ending: everything is perfectly recorded, and nothing can surprise anyone.
